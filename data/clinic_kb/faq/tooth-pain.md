# I Have a Toothache — What Should I Do?

**Q: I have a toothache. When should I call the dentist vs. just wait it out vs. go to the ER?**

A: Toothache severity matters. Here is a guide:

## Call Us During Business Hours If:
- Pain is mild to moderate and intermittent
- Sensitivity to hot, cold, or sweets that lingers for a few seconds
- Dull aching in a specific tooth
- A filling feels rough or loose but no severe pain

Call (555) 743-0200 — we'll schedule you as soon as possible.

## Call Our After-Hours Line (555-867-5309) If:
- Pain is severe, constant, or throbbing
- Pain wakes you from sleep
- Visible swelling of the gum around a tooth
- Pain accompanied by low-grade fever

## Go to the ER Immediately If:
- Your face, cheek, or neck is visibly swelling
- You have difficulty swallowing or breathing
- High fever (over 101°F) with facial swelling — this indicates a spreading infection that can become life-threatening

## Toothache Severity Quick Guide

| Symptom | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Mild sensitivity, no swelling | Schedule appointment this week |
| Moderate constant ache | Call us same day |
| Severe throbbing, swelling | After-hours emergency line |
| Swollen face + fever + difficulty swallowing | 911 / ER immediately |

## Temporary Home Relief While Waiting

- **OTC pain medication:** Ibuprofen (Advil/Motrin) is most effective for dental pain — take 400–600mg every 6–8 hours with food. Acetaminophen (Tylenol) can be alternated.
- **Clove oil:** Apply a small amount of clove oil to the painful area with a cotton ball — the eugenol provides temporary numbing relief
- **Cold pack:** Apply a cold pack to the outside of your cheek for 15–20 minutes to reduce swelling

## What NOT to Do
- Do not place aspirin directly on the gum tissue (causes chemical burns)
- Do not ignore a toothache and hope it resolves on its own — dental infections can spread
- Do not put off calling because you are anxious — we are here to help, not judge
